Comment resolution. The metal ion was found to be coordinated to the conserved residue His 183 |CITS: [9384565]|. Ferrochelatases are structurally well conserved, but the enzymes from Gram-positive organisms lack a small loop that forms one lip of the active site mouth. The absence of the sequence encoding this lip is sufficient to distinguish among genes that encode |FRAME: EC-4.99.1.1| and |FRAME: EC-4.99.1.c|. Specific interaction was observed between HemH and Fra, a frataxin homologue suggested to act as a regulatory component as well as an iron chaperone in different cellular pathways. It was found that Fra acts as a specific ferrous iron donor for HemH |CITS: [25826316]|.
|FRAME: EC-4.99.1.1|, catalyzes the insertion of Fe2+ into |FRAME: PROTOPORPHYRIN_IX|. The |FRAME: BSU10130| gene of |FRAME: TAX-1423| was thought to catalyze the reaction. The gene was cloned and expressed in |FRAME: TAX-562|, and the recombinant protein was purified. The enzyme was found to be a monomeric and water-soluble, and could incorporate not only Fe2+ but also Zn2+ and Cu2+ into protoporphyrin IX |CITS: [8119288]|. Subsequent studies showed that Tyr13 determines the metal specificity of the enzyme |CITS: [21052751][23097001]|. However, later studies have shown that the in vivo substrate of the enzyme from Gram-positive bacteria is not |FRAME: PROTOPORPHYRIN_IX| but |FRAME: COPROPORPHYRIN_III| |CITS: [25646457]|. For example, the Km of the enzyme from |FRAME: TAX-1773| for coproporphyrin III is over 60-fold lower than its Km for protoporphyrin IX. Anaerobic conditions were achieved in the stoppered flasks after a short time through the consumption of residual oxygen. Cells were harvested during the exponential growth phase.
